Third Latin Declension Exceptions

1. Pure I-Stems

Some nouns always or usually take -im in the Accusative singular and -ī in the Ablative singular:

  • Always: vīs (force), sitīs (thirst), tussis (cough), secūris (axe).
  • Usually: febris (fever), puppis (stern).

2. Irregular Nouns

These nouns have stem changes or irregular endings:

vīs (force): Nom: vīs, Acc: vim, Abl: vī | Pl: vīrēs, vīrium, vīribus...
bōs (ox/cow): Gen Pl: boum, Dat/Abl Pl: būbus (or bōbus)
Iuppiter (Jupiter): Gen: Iovis, Dat: Iovī, Acc: Iovem, Abl: Iove
iter (journey): Gen: itineris (stem: itiner-)
senex (old man): Gen: senis (stem: sen-)

3. Mixed Stems (Plural I-Stems)

Monosyllables in -s or -x following a consonant take -ium in the Genitive plural:

urbs → urbium
mons → montium
nox → noctium

Nouns in -is and -es that are not increasing (same syllables in Nom and Gen) also take -ium (e.g., hostis, caedēs).

4. Gender Exceptions

While rules often apply, watch out for these common exceptions:

  • Masculine in -is: collis (hill), mensis (month), piscis (fish), canis (dog).
  • Feminine in -os: arbōs (tree).
  • Masculine in -l/-n: sōl (sun), sal (salt), rēn (kidney).
