Second Latin Declension Exceptions

1. Feminine Nouns in -us

While most nouns in -us are masculine, many names of trees, towns, and countries are feminine:

  • Names of Trees: quercus (oak), pīnus (pine), fāgus (beech).
  • Names of Regions/Cities: Aegyptus (Egypt), Rhodos (Rhodes), Corinthus (Corinth).
  • Others: alvus (belly), colus (distaff), humus (ground).

2. Neuter Nouns in -us

A few nouns ending in -us are actually neuter (mostly found in the singular):

vulgus (the common people)
vīrus (poison / slime)
pelagus (the open sea)

These usually follow the neuter rule (Nom = Acc) but lack plural forms.

3. Vocative of Nouns in -ius

Proper names and nouns in -ius form the vocative by dropping the -us (ending in -ī):

fīlius (son) → fīlī
genius (spirit) → genī
Vergilius → Vergilī

Note: meus has the vocative mī.

4. The Irregular Noun: Deus (God)

The noun deus often has contracted forms in the plural:

Plural Nom: dī (rarely deī or diī)
Plural Gen: deōrum (or deum)
Plural Dat/Abl: dīs (rarely deīs or diīs)

5. Genitive Plural (-um)

Found primarily in poetry, or with words of weight, measure, and money:

  • deum (instead of deōrum)
  • nummum (instead of nummōrum)
  • talentum (instead of talentōrum)
